Leadership Profile Summary

Jordan operates through analytical frameworks, systems thinking, and a high-energy commitment to well-reasoned positions. The highest-confidence patterns: a tendency to diagnose structural causes rather than proximate ones, a decision style that gathers broadly and then anchors hard, and a communication gap between the precision Jordan intends and the impatience others experience.

The most productive development territory sits at the intersection of two findings: the hypothesis-anchoring pattern in decisions, and the Leadership Identity gap between stated intention (developing independent decision-makers) and observed behavior (high analytical investment, lower relational investment). These are related — a leader who anchors to hypotheses in product decisions may also be anchoring to a model of leadership that doesn't quite match how they show up in practice.

The self-awareness is genuine and specific. Jordan names hard things directly in the reflection, which both validates the methodology and suggests the development work will be productive. The readiness is there. The question is whether the identity gap gets addressed as directly as the decision style gap — one is cognitive, the other is relational, and analytical leaders typically find the cognitive work more accessible.

Constructs with insufficient evidence: Leadership Identity (stated vs. observed gap requires more relational behavioral data to evaluate fully). Communication patterns under verbal/conflict conditions (corpus captures written communication only).

Leadership User Manual — Jordan Reyes

Excerpt — approximately half of a full manual. Sections shown: How I Think, Where I Create Friction, and My Governing Questions.

First person Shareable Excerpt — ~half of full length
Section — How I Think

I think in structures. When I encounter a problem, my instinct is to ask what system is producing it before I ask who is responsible for it or what solution would fix it. A feature request is usually a symptom of a workflow problem. A workflow problem is usually a symptom of an incentive misalignment or a feedback loop that isn't closing. I work backward from the structural root before I consider the surface interventions.

This makes me most effective in problems that have structural causes and most frustrated in problems that don't — where the situation genuinely requires a relational fix or a fast judgment call rather than a diagnostic framework. I have learned to notice when I'm applying analytical tools to problems that need something else. I don't always catch it in time.

I form hypotheses quickly and defend them with high energy. This is often a strength — I move faster to a reasoned position than most people around me. It is a liability when the hypothesis is wrong and I've already invested in it. I know this about myself. The challenge is that the energy I bring to defending a good hypothesis looks identical, from the outside, to the energy I bring to defending a wrong one.

Section — Where I Create Friction

Hypothesis anchoring. I gather information broadly before committing to a position. Once I've committed, I update reluctantly. The problem is not that I'm wrong more often than average — it's that when I'm wrong, I stay wrong longer than I should. The trigger is usually when I've already socialized a position with stakeholders. Once I've said it out loud in a room, the cost of revising feels higher than it actually is.

The communication gap. I write and speak in complete, structured arguments. Most of the time, I think this is respectful — I'm telling you what I actually think in the most precise way I can. Some people experience it as intensity or impatience. I've been told I can make people feel like they need to have their argument fully formed before they talk to me. That's not my intention. I'm working on creating more space for thinking-in-progress, especially in 1:1s.

Relational investment. I care about the people on my team. I am not always good at showing it in the ways that matter to them. I default to engaging with people's work rather than with them as people — asking what they're building before asking how they are. This is a pattern I notice more in retrospect than in the moment.

Section — My Governing Questions

These are the questions I run consequential decisions through, extracted from how I've actually made decisions rather than how I'd describe them if asked:

"What is this metric actually measuring — and is that the thing we want to change?" I apply this to almost every data-informed decision. The metric is usually fine. The interpretation of the metric is where the errors live.

"What would have to be true for this to be wrong?" This is the question I try to ask before committing to a product direction. I am better at asking it in theory than in practice — but asking it at all has caught more mistakes than I can count.

"Is this the right problem, or just the problem that showed up?" The problems that show up in product are usually symptoms. This question is a forcing function to look one level up before I start solving.

→
What makes a strong User Manual: First person throughout. Friction section that is honest rather than performatively humble — "I stay wrong longer than I should" is more useful than "I have high standards." Governing questions that feel extracted from behavioral evidence rather than assembled from self-image. The test: would a new collaborator who read this understand something genuinely different about how to work with this person?

Working-With-Me Guide — Jordan Reyes

One page. Five sections. Full document — not an excerpt. Readable in under 3 minutes.

One page Shareable Full document shown

To bring me a problem: Frame it as a question, not a solution pitch. Tell me what you've already ruled out and why — that's the most useful context. If you don't know what you want from me (a decision, a thought partner, a resource), say that upfront. I'll move faster if I know which role to play. You don't need a fully formed position to come to me — but bring real data or a specific example, not a general concern.

To challenge me effectively: Show me what I missed, not just that you disagree. I will update my position when the evidence warrants it — I am genuinely open to being wrong, even on things I've committed to publicly. The most effective challenges are specific: "You assumed X, but here's data that suggests Y." The least effective: repeated general pushback without new information. If I seem to have stopped engaging with your argument, ask me directly what's not landing.

What I need from you: Specificity — in problems, in feedback, in status updates. I work better with a precise question than a broad topic. Follow-through: if you commit to something, I'm tracking it even if I don't say so. And patience when I'm in diagnostic mode — sometimes the framework-building is the work, not a detour from it.

What you can expect from me: A structured, reasoned position on almost any question you bring me. Direct feedback that names the specific thing, not a softened version of it. Investment in your thinking — I genuinely want to understand how you got to your conclusion, not just what it is. High expectations, applied to myself as much as to you. I will tell you when I think you're wrong, and I expect the same in return.

When I'm struggling — what to watch for: I get quieter and more precise. If I start responding to broad questions with very narrow, technical answers, I've probably retreated into a comfort zone. I also tend to over-prepare when I'm anxious — if I'm sending very long pre-reads before routine meetings, something is off. The best way to check in is directly: "You seem like you're in your head about something — are you?"

→
What makes a strong Working-With-Me Guide: Every sentence specific enough that it could not apply to any other leader. The struggling signals section names actual behavioral patterns ("very long pre-reads before routine meetings"), not emotional states. The "to challenge me" section acknowledges the specific mechanism that blocks effective challenge — then explains how to get through it anyway.

AI Calibration Document — Jordan Reyes

One page. Paste at the start of any new AI conversation. Full document shown.

One page Paste into AI tools Full document shown

My communication style: Structured and argument-driven. I think in frameworks and I write in complete sentences with traceable logic. Bullet-point summaries that skip the connective reasoning frustrate me — I want to see why each point follows. Generic outputs are immediately obvious to me; the fastest way to produce something I'll use is to be more specific than you think you need to be. If you're uncertain about something, name the uncertainty rather than smoothing over it.

My operating principles: Structural diagnosis before surface solution — I want to understand what system is producing a problem before I consider how to fix it. Hypothesis integrity — I'd rather surface a weak hypothesis early than commit to one that doesn't hold. Evidence before advocacy — I will give you my position, but I want it grounded in something more than confidence. Specificity as respect — vague feedback or vague questions are a form of inefficiency I'd rather avoid on both sides.

How to give me feedback: Direct and specific. "This section doesn't follow from the previous one because X" is useful. "This feels a bit off" is not. I'm not fragile — name the problem precisely and I'll fix it or explain why I disagree. If I've made an assumption you think is wrong, cite the specific assumption and what you think I missed.

What good output looks like: The argument is traceable — I can follow the reasoning from premise to conclusion without inferring missing steps. The format matches the complexity: simple question, concise answer; complex problem, developed framework. Draft means a real draft — not a template with placeholders or hedged generalities I'll have to rewrite entirely.

My governing question: "What is this metric actually measuring — and is that the thing we want to change?" Apply this when we're working from data, when evaluating options, or when I ask you to help me assess a situation. I am more interested in what the evidence actually shows than in confirmation of a position I've already taken.

→
What makes a strong AI Calibration Document: The governing question section is the highest-leverage element — it shapes how the AI frames tradeoffs in every subsequent output. The "what good output looks like" section should be specific enough to actually change AI behavior, not just describe preferences. Test: paste this at the start of a conversation. Does the AI's first response feel different than it would have otherwise?

Development Roadmap — Jordan Reyes

Private. Not for sharing. Excerpt showing two of five development priorities and the 90-day focus.

Private — do not share Update every 90 days Excerpt shown
Development Priorities — 2 of 5 shown

Priority 1: Hypothesis anchoring in post-commitment decisions

The mechanism: Jordan gathers evidence broadly before forming a position — this phase is genuinely open. But once a hypothesis is formed and socialized, updating it becomes cognitively costly. The cost isn't the evidence review — it's the social cost of being seen to reverse. This means corrections happen late, after the wrong direction has been traveled some distance.

The trigger: Any decision that has been shared with stakeholders before the evidence is fully in. The socialization is the trigger for anchoring — it converts a hypothesis into a commitment before it has earned that status.

The practice: Before socializing any product direction, explicitly designate it as a hypothesis with a named confidence level and a stated condition for updating. ("I think X is the right prioritization — I'm at 70% confidence, and I'd update if we saw Y.") This externalizes the uncertainty in a way that makes later revision feel like scientific process rather than failure.

Priority 2: Relational investment with direct reports

The mechanism: Jordan's default engagement mode is work-first: asking what someone is building before asking how they are. This is efficient and often appropriate. It becomes a friction point in situations where a direct report needs to be seen as a person before they can engage productively on the work — which is more often than Jordan currently accounts for.

The trigger:1:1 meetings, especially when the direct report has signaled (or should have signaled) something personal or difficult is happening. Jordan often misses these signals because the work frame is already active by the time the meeting starts.

The practice: For the first 2 minutes of every 1:1, there is no agenda. The only question is some version of "how are you?" and the only rule is that Jordan doesn't respond to the answer with a segue to the work. This sounds small. It is not. It changes the relational texture of the conversation before the work content begins, and the people Jordan leads will feel its presence or absence clearly.

90-Day Focus

The leadership identity gap. Not the hypothesis anchoring — Jordan already understands that pattern intellectually. The more important 90-day work is closing the gap between the leader Jordan describes wanting to be (someone who develops independent decision-makers) and the leader the evidence shows Jordan currently is (high analytical investment, lower relational and developmental investment).

The specific practice: identify one direct report who is operating below their ceiling and have an explicit development conversation — not about their current project, but about where they want to be in 18 months and what Jordan can do to support that trajectory. Do this before the end of the 90-day period. The point is not the outcome of that conversation. The point is building the muscle of having it.

→
What makes a strong Development Roadmap: Priorities that name the mechanism specifically — not "be more open to feedback" but "the socialization of a hypothesis converts it into a commitment before it's earned that status." Practices concrete enough to actually do on a specific day. A 90-day focus that chooses the most important thing rather than the most comfortable one.
