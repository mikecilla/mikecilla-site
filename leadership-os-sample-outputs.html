Profile Summary — evidence-informed hypotheses

Across the synthetic evidence sources, Jordan's profile suggests a leader who operates primarily through systems framing, structured communication, and a strong analytical investment in product decisions. The highest-confidence patterns — Systems Orientation and Decision Style — are well-evidenced across all three modalities and point to a coherent cognitive signature: Jordan diagnoses structure before intervening, gathers broadly before committing, and holds hard to a direction once it has been socialized.

The most generative development territory appears at the intersection of two moderate-confidence patterns: the post-commitment anchoring in product decisions, and the Leadership Identity gap between the stated aspiration (developing independent decision-makers) and the observed behavioral emphasis (analytical over relational investment). These may be related — a leader who anchors to product hypotheses may similarly anchor to a model of effective leadership that does not yet include the relational and developmental behaviors the identity aspires to.

Self-Awareness appears genuine and specific within the domains Jordan monitors. The hypothesis worth examining is whether there are adjacent domains — particularly interpersonal accommodation over time — where the self-model is less accurate than Jordan currently believes.

Constructs with insufficient evidence for confident hypothesis generation: Leadership Identity (requires more relational behavioral observation). Communication patterns under verbal, informal, or high-stakes conflict conditions (written corpus cannot speak to this).

Leadership User Manual — Jordan Reyes

Synthetic illustrative example. Excerpt — three of six sections. Full document is 3–4 pages, written in first person, designed to be shared with a manager, team member, or key collaborator.

First person Shareable Excerpt · illustrative only
How I think

I think in structures. My first instinct when encountering a product problem is to look for the operating model failure before I look at the feature gap. A roadmap that keeps churning usually isn't a prioritization problem — it's a signal that the decision rights between Product, Engineering, and the business aren't clear enough to hold. I work upstream before I work on the surface.

I form hypotheses quickly and commit to them with energy. This helps me move a team toward a direction when ambiguity is high. It creates friction when the hypothesis is wrong and I've already aligned people to it — the social cost of reversing makes me hold on longer than the evidence warrants. I know this about myself and I'm working on naming hypotheses explicitly before socializing them.

Where I create friction

Post-commitment anchoring. Once I've socialized a product direction, I revise reluctantly. The problem isn't that I ignore contradictory evidence — it's that I weight the social cost of reversal too heavily relative to the cost of staying wrong. The practice I'm building: label a direction as a hypothesis with an explicit confidence level before sharing it, so revision later reads as learning rather than failure.

Cross-functional communication gap. I write and present in complete, structured arguments. Partners in Sales and Customer Success have told me this reads as impatience. I experience it as efficiency. I'm working on making space for partial thinking earlier in a conversation — signaling that ambiguity is welcome before the argument is formed, not just after it.

Relational investment. I invest heavily in the analytical work of product leadership. I invest less consistently in the relational and developmental work — checking in on how my direct reports are doing, having explicit career conversations, noticing what's happening under the surface. This is a pattern I see more clearly in retrospect than in the moment.

My governing questions

These appear to drive how I evaluate decisions — extracted from patterns in how I actually reason, not from how I'd describe my decision-making in the abstract:

"Does this roadmap reflect what customers actually need, or what we find technically interesting?" I use this to interrupt the pull toward elegant architecture over useful product.

"Who needs to make this decision, and am I helping them make it or making it for them?" I apply this when I notice I've been the decision-maker on something that should have been someone else's call to build.

"What would have to be true for this hypothesis to be wrong — and have I actually checked?" This is the question I'm trying to ask before committing to a direction, not after.

Governing questions that feel extracted from behavioral evidence, not assembled from self-image. First person throughout. A document specific enough that someone who read it would know something genuinely useful about how to work with this person.

Working-With-Me Guide — Jordan Reyes

Synthetic illustrative example. Full document. Readable in under 3 minutes. Designed to be shared with any collaborator.

To bring me a problem
Frame it as a question rather than a recommendation. Tell me what you've already ruled out and why — that context is more useful than a polished pitch. If you don't know what you need from me (a decision, a thought partner, a resource connection), say that upfront. You don't need a complete argument — but anchor it in a specific example or data point, not a general pattern.
To challenge me effectively
Show me what I missed, not just that you disagree. Specific contradictory evidence is far more effective than persistent general pushback. I am genuinely open to revising a direction — but I need to see what the evidence actually shows, not just that you feel differently. If I seem to have stopped engaging with your argument, ask me directly: "What's not landing for you here?"
What I need from you
Specificity in problems, feedback, and status. I work better with a precise question than a general topic. Follow-through: if you commit to something in our conversation, I'm tracking it. And some patience when I'm in diagnostic mode — the upstream analysis is often the actual work, not a delay before the work starts.
What you can expect from me
A structured position on almost any question you bring. Direct feedback that names the specific thing — not a softened version of it. Genuine investment in understanding how you got to your conclusion, not just what it is. High expectations applied to my own work as much as to yours. I will tell you when I think you're wrong, and I expect the same.
When I'm struggling — what to watch for
I get more precise and narrower when something is off — responding to broad questions with very specific, technical answers is usually a sign I've retreated somewhere comfortable. I also tend to over-prepare for routine conversations when I'm anxious about them — unusually detailed pre-reads before a standard sync is a signal. The most direct check-in works best: "You seem like you're in your head about something."

My communication style
Structured and argument-driven. I think in product systems and write with traceable logic — show me why each conclusion follows from what preceded it. Bullet-point summaries that skip the connective reasoning frustrate me; I want to see the argument, not just its conclusions. Generic outputs are immediately obvious. The fastest way to produce something useful is to be more specific than you think necessary. If you're uncertain, name the uncertainty rather than smoothing it.
My operating principles
Operating model before roadmap — I want to understand the structural constraint before I consider the feature-level fix. Hypothesis integrity — label working hypotheses explicitly so they can be revised without social cost. Customer evidence before product intuition — I want to see what the data actually shows before I advocate for a direction. Specificity as respect — vague problems and vague feedback are inefficiency I'd rather avoid on both sides.
How to give me feedback
Direct and specific. "This section doesn't follow from the previous one because X" is useful. "This feels off" is not. I'm not fragile about being wrong — name the specific problem and I'll engage with it directly. If I've made an assumption you think is wrong, name the assumption and what I missed. Don't soften critical feedback into ambiguity.
What good output looks like
The logic is traceable — I can follow reasoning from premise to conclusion without inferring missing steps. Format matches complexity: concise for simple questions, developed framework for complex ones. Draft means a real draft — not a template or placeholder I have to substantially rewrite. If the question is about a product decision, start with what the evidence shows, not with what sounds reasonable.
My governing question

"Does this roadmap reflect what customers actually need, or what we find technically interesting?" Apply this when we're evaluating product options, framing tradeoffs, or assessing whether a direction is evidence-led or hypothesis-led. I'm more interested in what the customer data actually shows than in confirming a direction I've already taken.

Development Roadmap — Jordan Reyes

Synthetic illustrative example. Private — not for sharing. Excerpt showing two of five development priorities and the 90-day focus.

Private · do not share Update every 90 days Excerpt · illustrative only
Priority 1 — Post-commitment anchoring

The mechanism: Jordan's information-gathering phase is genuinely open. Once a product direction has been socialized with stakeholders, however, revision becomes socially costly — and the cost appears to suppress updating even when new evidence would warrant it. The issue is not analytical rigidity; it is the premature conversion of a working hypothesis into a social commitment.

The trigger: Any product direction that has been shared with the broader team or leadership before the supporting evidence is complete. Socialization is the trigger — it closes the hypothesis before it has been tested.

The practice: Before socializing any product direction, designate it explicitly as a hypothesis with a stated confidence level and a named condition for updating it. "I think X is the right prioritization — I'm at 65% confidence, and I'd revise if we saw Y from the customer data." This makes later revision feel like scientific process rather than leadership failure.

Priority 2 — Relational investment with direct reports

The mechanism: Jordan's default engagement mode is work-first: asking what someone is building before asking how they are. This is often appropriate and efficient. It becomes a development edge in situations where a direct report needs to be seen as a person — particularly when something difficult is happening outside the immediate work — before they can engage fully on the work itself.

The trigger: 1:1 meetings, especially when a direct report has given a quiet signal that something is off. Jordan tends to miss these signals because the work frame is already active before the conversation starts.

The practice: In the first two minutes of every 1:1, there is no agenda. The only question is some version of "how are you?" and the only constraint is not immediately redirecting the answer toward the work. This adjustment is smaller than it sounds and larger in effect than expected.

90-day focus

The leadership identity gap. The post-commitment anchoring pattern is well understood intellectually and has a named practice attached to it. The more important 90-day work is beginning to close the gap between the leader Jordan describes wanting to be — someone who develops independent decision-makers — and the leader the evidence suggests Jordan currently is, with high analytical investment and lower developmental investment in direct reports.

The specific practice: identify one direct report who appears to be operating below their potential ceiling and initiate an explicit development conversation — not about their current sprint or project, but about where they want to be in 18 months and what Jordan could do to support that trajectory. Complete this before the end of the 90-day period. The goal is not a particular outcome from that conversation. The goal is building the cognitive and relational habit of having it.

What distinguishes a strong illustrative Development Roadmap: Priorities that name the mechanism specifically — not "delegate more" but "the social cost of revision after socialization suppresses updating." Practices concrete enough to perform on a specific day. A 90-day focus that names the most important development territory, not the most comfortable one.
