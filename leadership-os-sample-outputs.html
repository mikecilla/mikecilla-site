Leadership User Manual — Jordan Reyes

Excerpt — three of six sections. Full document is 3–4 pages first-person, structured for sharing with a new manager or key collaborator.

First person Shareable Excerpt shown
How I Think

I think in structures. When I encounter a problem, my instinct is to ask what system is producing it before I ask who is responsible or what solution would fix it. A feature request is usually a symptom of a workflow problem. A workflow problem is usually a symptom of an incentive misalignment or a feedback loop that isn't closing. I work backward from the structural root before I consider surface interventions.

I form hypotheses quickly and defend them with high energy. This is often a strength — I move faster to a reasoned position than most people around me. It is a liability when the hypothesis is wrong and I've already invested in it. The challenge is that the energy I bring to defending a good hypothesis looks identical, from the outside, to the energy I bring to defending a wrong one.

Where I Create Friction

Hypothesis anchoring. I gather information broadly before committing to a position. Once I've committed, I update reluctantly — especially when I've already socialized the position. The cost of revising feels higher than it actually is. I've learned to name hypotheses explicitly before socializing them. I don't always remember.

The communication gap. I write and speak in complete, structured arguments. Some people experience this as intensity or impatience. I've been told I can make people feel they need a fully formed argument before coming to me. That's not my intention. I'm working on creating more space for thinking-in-progress, especially in 1:1s.

Relational investment. I care about the people on my team. I am not always good at showing it in the ways that matter to them — I default to engaging with people's work rather than with them as people. This is a pattern I notice more in retrospect than in the moment.

My Governing Questions

"What is this metric actually measuring — and is that the thing we want to change?" I apply this to almost every data-informed decision. The metric is usually fine. The interpretation is where errors live.

"What would have to be true for this to be wrong?" This is what I try to ask before committing to a product direction. I am better at asking it in theory than in practice — but asking it at all has caught more mistakes than I can count.

"Is this the right problem, or just the problem that showed up?" The problems that show up in product are usually symptoms. This is a forcing function to look one level up before I start solving.

→
Strong User Manual signal: Friction section that is honest rather than performatively humble — "I stay wrong longer than I should" is more useful than "I have high standards." Governing questions that feel extracted from behavioral evidence, not assembled from self-image.

Working-With-Me Guide — Jordan Reyes

Full document. Five sections. Readable in under 3 minutes. Designed to be shared with any collaborator.

One page Shareable Full document
To bring me a problem
Frame it as a question, not a solution pitch. Tell me what you've ruled out and why — that's the most useful context. If you don't know what you want from me (a decision, a thought partner, a resource), say that upfront. You don't need a fully formed position. But bring real data or a specific example, not a general concern.
To challenge me effectively
Show me what I missed, not just that you disagree. I will update my position when the evidence warrants it — I'm genuinely open to being wrong, even on things I've committed to publicly. The most effective challenges are specific: "You assumed X, but here's data that suggests Y." Repeated general pushback without new information is the least effective approach.
What I need from you
Specificity — in problems, in feedback, in status updates. I work better with a precise question than a broad topic. Follow-through: if you commit to something, I'm tracking it even if I don't say so. And patience when I'm in diagnostic mode — sometimes the framework-building is the work, not a detour from it.
What you can expect from me
A structured, reasoned position on almost any question you bring me. Direct feedback that names the specific thing, not a softened version of it. Investment in your thinking — I genuinely want to understand how you got to your conclusion, not just what it is. High expectations, applied to myself as much as to you. I will tell you when I think you're wrong, and I expect the same.
When I'm struggling — what to watch for
I get quieter and more precise. If I start responding to broad questions with very narrow, technical answers, I've probably retreated into a comfort zone. I also tend to over-prepare when I'm anxious — if I'm sending very long pre-reads before routine meetings, something is off. The best way to check in is directly: "You seem like you're in your head about something — are you?"
→
Strong Working-With-Me signal: Every sentence specific enough that it could not apply to any other leader. Struggling signals name actual behavioral patterns ("very long pre-reads before routine meetings"), not emotional states. The "to challenge me" section acknowledges the specific blocking mechanism — then explains how to get around it.

My communication style
Structured and argument-driven. I think in frameworks and write in complete sentences with traceable logic. Bullet-point summaries that skip the connective reasoning frustrate me — I want to see why each point follows. Generic outputs are immediately obvious; the fastest way to produce something I'll use is to be more specific than you think necessary. If you're uncertain about something, name the uncertainty rather than smoothing over it.
My operating principles
Structural diagnosis before surface solution — I want to understand what system produces a problem before considering how to fix it. Hypothesis integrity — surface a weak hypothesis early rather than commit to one that won't hold. Evidence before advocacy — I'll give you my position, but I want it grounded in something more than confidence. Specificity as respect — vague feedback or vague questions are inefficiency I'd rather avoid on both sides.
How to give me feedback
Direct and specific. "This section doesn't follow from the previous one because X" is useful. "This feels a bit off" is not. I'm not fragile — name the problem precisely and I'll fix it or explain why I disagree. If I've made an assumption you think is wrong, cite the specific assumption and what I missed.
What good output looks like
The argument is traceable — I can follow the reasoning from premise to conclusion without inferring missing steps. Format matches complexity: simple question, concise answer; complex problem, developed framework. Draft means a real draft — not a template with placeholders or hedged generalities I'll have to rewrite entirely.
My governing question

"What is this metric actually measuring — and is that the thing we want to change?" Apply this when we're working from data, when evaluating options, or when I ask you to assess a situation. I am more interested in what the evidence actually shows than in confirmation of a position I've already taken.

→
Strong AI Calibration signal: The governing question section is the highest-leverage element — it shapes how the AI frames tradeoffs in every subsequent output. Test: paste this at the start of a conversation. Does the first response feel different than it would have otherwise?

Development Roadmap — Jordan Reyes

Private. Not for sharing. Excerpt — two of five priorities, plus 90-day focus.

Private — do not share Update every 90 days Excerpt shown
Priority 1 — Hypothesis anchoring

The mechanism: Jordan gathers evidence broadly before forming a position — genuinely open. But once a hypothesis is formed and socialized, updating it becomes costly. The cost isn't the evidence review — it's the social cost of being seen to reverse. Corrections happen late, after the wrong direction has been traveled some distance.

The trigger: Socializing a position with stakeholders before the evidence is fully in. This converts a hypothesis into a commitment before it's earned that status.

The practice: Before socializing any direction, designate it explicitly as a hypothesis with a stated confidence level and a named condition for updating. "I think X is right — I'm at 70%, and I'd update if we saw Y." This externalizes the uncertainty in a way that makes later revision feel like scientific process rather than failure.

Priority 2 — Relational investment

The mechanism: Jordan's default engagement mode is work-first: asking what someone is building before asking how they are. Efficient and often appropriate. Becomes a friction point when a direct report needs to be seen as a person before they can engage productively — which is more often than Jordan currently accounts for.

The trigger: 1:1 meetings, especially when a direct report has signaled (or should have signaled) something personal or difficult. Jordan often misses these signals because the work frame is already active by the time the meeting starts.

The practice: First 2 minutes of every 1:1: no agenda. The only question is some version of "how are you?" and the only rule is not responding to the answer with a segue to work. This sounds small. It changes the relational texture of the entire conversation.

90-Day Focus

The leadership identity gap. Not the hypothesis anchoring — Jordan already understands that intellectually. The more important 90-day work is closing the gap between the leader Jordan describes wanting to be (someone who develops independent decision-makers) and the leader the evidence shows Jordan currently is (high analytical investment, lower relational and developmental investment).

The specific practice: identify one direct report who is operating below their ceiling and have an explicit development conversation — not about their current project, but about where they want to be in 18 months and what Jordan can do to support that trajectory. Do this before the end of the 90-day period. The point is not the outcome of that conversation. The point is building the muscle of having it.

→
Strong Development Roadmap signal: Priorities that name the mechanism specifically. Practices concrete enough to do on a specific day. A 90-day focus that chooses the most important thing, not the most comfortable one. This document should be harder to read than the User Manual. If it isn't, the synthesis was too gentle.
