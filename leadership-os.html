Leadership Operating System.

Most leadership assessments tell you what you're like. This one tells you how you actually work.

The Leadership OS is a structured methodology for making your leadership patterns explicit — using personality science, behavioral evidence from your AI conversation history, and guided reflection. The output is a set of working documents you'll actually use.

Why this exists

The assessment
isn't the
problem.

Most leaders have taken a personality assessment at some point. Probably more than one. And most of them remember the results as accurate, interesting, and largely unused six months later.

The report becomes a document. The insight fades. The development doesn't happen.

The Leadership OS is built on a different premise: that what matters isn't the assessment itself, but whether the insights get integrated into how you actually work. That requires something assessments don't provide — a living document you return to, behavioral evidence grounded in your real patterns, and a system for ongoing reflection rather than a one-time report.

This methodology combines validated personality science with a genuinely novel input: the behavioral evidence already embedded in your AI conversation history. The result is a profile derived from how you actually think and decide — not just how you describe yourself.

Most free personality assessments produce output that's either too abstract to be actionable (Big Five alone) or too interpretation-dependent to be credible (MBTI-adjacent instruments). PrinciplesYou sits at the useful intersection: validated trait science presented in language leaders can actually use. It's also genuinely free and takes 20 minutes rather than 90. Step 3 in detail

The six
reflection prompts.

Answer these as specifically as you can. Real situations, actual decisions, honest observations. The more concrete the input, the more useful the output. Avoid generalities — the interesting material is always in the specific story.

Prompt 01 · Peak Performance
"Describe a specific situation where you were operating at your best as a leader. What were you doing, who were you with, what were the conditions — and what made it different from ordinary work?"
This surfaces your Flow conditions — the specific combination of challenge, context, and collaboration that produces your best work. The details matter more than the summary. What was the challenge level? Who was involved? What kind of problem were you solving?
Prompt 02 · Decision Process
"Describe a decision you made in the past year that you're genuinely proud of. Not the outcome — the process. How did you get there?"
Most people default to describing outcomes rather than process. This prompt forces attention to the actual decision mechanics: how you gathered information, stress-tested assumptions, managed uncertainty, and committed to a direction. The process reveals far more about how you operate than the outcome does.
Prompt 03 · A Decision You'd Make Differently
"Describe a decision or situation you'd handle differently if you could do it again. What happened, what did you do, and what does that tell you about how you operate under pressure or uncertainty?"
This is where the most useful development material lives. Not failure — just honest reflection on the gap between how you wanted to operate and how you actually did. The pattern across multiple examples like this reveals the specific conditions under which your defaults work against you.
Prompt 04 · Recurring Frustration
"What's a recurring frustration in how you work — something that comes up repeatedly with other people, with organizations, or with yourself? Be specific about what triggers it and how it tends to show up."
Recurring frustrations are diagnostic. They reveal the gap between how you expect things to work and how they actually do — which tells you something important about your standards, your defaults, and the environments where you're most and least effective. The specificity of the trigger matters as much as the frustration itself.
Prompt 05 · Energy Sources
"When do you find the work genuinely energizing? Not 'meaningful work' in the abstract — specific contexts, activities, types of people, kinds of problems. What's happening when you lose track of time?"
Generic answers here (I love building things, I care about people) are interesting but not diagnostic. The useful version names specific conditions: the type of problem, the stage of the work, who else is in the room, what the stakes are. This is the input for your Flow profile — the conditions that produce your highest-quality engagement.
Prompt 06 · The Misread
"What do people consistently get wrong about you — as a leader, as a colleague, as a person? What does that misread tell you about the gap between how you operate and how you come across?"
This is the most revealing prompt in the set. The gap between intent and impact is where most leadership development work actually happens. People who can articulate this clearly have usually done real self-reflection. People who say "I don't think people misread me" are telling you something too.
Step 2 in detail

Your AI has been
watching you
work.

If you've used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any AI tool with any regularity, you've accumulated something you probably haven't thought about: a behavioral record. Thousands of questions, decisions, communications, and challenges — documented in a way that no other medium captures.

Every time you worked through a hard decision with your AI, you left evidence of how you actually think. Every communication you drafted, every problem you brought, every frustration you expressed — behavioral data, accumulated over time.

The Corpus Audit Prompt asks your AI to reflect on that record and identify patterns. It's not asking it to summarize conversations. It's asking it to describe what it has observed about how you operate — the same thing a thoughtful colleague might say after working with you closely for a year.

Copy the prompt below and paste it directly into your AI tool. Read what comes back with an open mind. Then copy that output into your synthesis prompt alongside your PrinciplesYou results and your reflection prompt answers.

The Corpus Audit Prompt — copy and paste into your AI tool

I'd like you to reflect on our conversation history together. Don't summarize specific conversations. Instead, identify behavioral patterns — things you've observed repeatedly about how I think, work, and communicate.

Please reflect on the following and share what you've noticed:

1. How do I tend to frame problems? What patterns do you see in how I approach complex situations?

2. What topics or domains come up most frequently? Where does my thinking seem deepest?

3. How would you describe my communication style — how I ask questions, express disagreement, and respond to your outputs?

4. What seems to energize me? What do I gravitate toward?

5. What frustrates me? Where do you see friction or impatience in how I engage?

6. What recurring questions or challenges keep coming up?

7. What are my decision-making tendencies — how I evaluate tradeoffs, what I prioritize, what I avoid?

8. What do you notice about my relationship to structure, ambiguity, detail, and systems?

Be specific and honest. I'm not looking for flattery. I'm looking for an accurate behavioral profile based on what you've actually observed.

Works with ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any AI tool with conversation memory. Best results with 3+ months of regular use. If you're new to AI tools, skip this step and return to it in a few months — the profile will only get richer.
What you get

Three documents.
All of them useful.

Once you've completed all three inputs, you'll run a synthesis prompt in your AI tool that produces three working documents — not a report, not a summary, but documents you'll actually return to.

01
Leadership User Manual
3–4 pages. How you think, how you decide, what you need to do your best work. Written in first person. Designed to be shared — with a new manager, a new team, or a key collaborator.
  • Cognitive signature and decision patterns
  • Flow conditions — what produces your best work
  • Development edges — where your defaults create friction
  • Governing questions extracted from behavioral evidence
02
Working-With-Me Guide
1 page. The practical version of the above. What to bring me, how to challenge me effectively, what I need, what I'll give you, and what to watch for when I'm struggling.
  • How to bring me a problem
  • How to challenge me effectively
  • What I need from collaborators
  • The signals that I'm struggling
03
AI Calibration Document
1 page. The document you give your AI assistant so it can work with you effectively — voice, preferences, operating principles, what good output looks like to you.
  • Communication style and voice guide
  • What you find valuable vs. generic
  • Operating principles and non-negotiables
  • How to calibrate responses over time
About this framework

Built from
an experiment
I ran on myself.

I didn't set out to build a leadership methodology. I was trying to make my AI tools more useful at work.

Over 2.5 years as a VP of People Operations, I accumulated hundreds of working conversations with AI — workforce analytics, HR system design, org design, business cases, leadership decisions. At some point I realized I'd accidentally built something: a detailed record of how I actually think.

When I transferred that context to a new AI tool, I had to make my thinking explicit in ways it had never been before. What came out of that process was the most accurate leadership profile I'd ever received — more specific than any 360, more honest than most coaching engagements, grounded in actual behavioral evidence rather than survey responses.

The Leadership OS is an attempt to make that process replicable — for anyone, using whatever AI tools they already have, in about 75 minutes.

Background M.S. Industrial-Organizational Psychology, San Jose State University
Experience 15 years in People Operations, workforce analytics, and HR technology
Grounding Big Five / HEXACO personality science, I/O Psychology, Flow theory
What I learned
"I was attempting to train an AI. The AI ended up training me. Not in a mystical sense — in a very practical one. Making my thinking legible to a system that needed explicit context forced a level of self-reflection that accumulated organizational knowledge never demands."
The I/O Psychology observation
"Every existing leadership assessment asks people to describe themselves. The corpus audit asks the system that has watched them work to describe what it saw. That's a different kind of evidence."
On validation
"This is not a validated assessment methodology — there's no normative sample, no inter-rater reliability. I want to be honest about what it isn't. But as a way of generating self-knowledge, it produced something I haven't gotten from any formal process."
