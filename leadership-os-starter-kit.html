Leadership OS Starter Kit · v1.0

Complete your
Leadership OS.

A self-guided process. Seven steps. About 125 minutes. You leave with four working documents about how you lead.

Total time ~125 minutes Sessions One sitting recommended Outputs 4 working documents

What this is

Leadership OS is a structured process for understanding how you actually operate as a leader — not how you describe yourself, but how you behave, decide, and communicate in practice.

It combines three inputs: a personality assessment, a behavioral audit of your AI conversation history, and six structured reflection prompts. Your AI tool synthesizes these into working documents you can use, share, and update over time.

What you will produce

Document 01
Leadership User Manual
3–4 pages. How you think, decide, engage, and lead. First person. Designed to be shared with a manager, team member, or collaborator.
Document 02
Working-With-Me Guide
1 page. The practical version. Readable in under 3 minutes. What to bring you, how to challenge you, what you need, what to watch for.
Document 03
AI Calibration Document
1 page. Teaches any AI assistant how you work. Paste it at the start of any new AI conversation to accelerate calibration.
Document 04 · Private
Development Roadmap
2–3 pages. Your private guide to where intentional practice would produce the most return. Not for sharing.

What this is not

Not a personality test. The outputs are not scores or types. They are working documents.

Not executive coaching or therapy. Leadership OS produces self-knowledge. What you do with it is up to you. If anything in this process surfaces something that feels clinical or distressing, set it aside and talk to a professional.

Not a validated psychometric instrument. The outputs are hypotheses grounded in evidence — not scientifically validated findings. Some will be accurate immediately. Some will need correction. Your job is to notice which is which.

What you will need

  • 90–125 minutes of uninterrupted time (one sitting recommended)
  • Access to PrinciplesYou — free at principlesyou.com
  • Access to at least one AI tool: ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini
  • Somewhere to save your outputs: Google Docs, Notes, or a folder on your computer

A note on quality

The quality of your Leadership OS outputs depends almost entirely on the quality of your inputs. Vague reflection responses produce generic documents. Specific, honest responses produce a profile that surprises you with its accuracy.

The reflection section asks for real situations — specific examples with enough detail to be useful. That specificity is the work. Do not rush it.

Inputs.

Section 02 · 15 minutes

Corpus
Audit.

Behavioral evidence from how you actually work. Not what you say about yourself — what a system that watched you work observed about you.

What corpus evidence is

If you use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any AI tool regularly, you have accumulated a behavioral record. Every decision you worked through, every communication you drafted, every problem you framed — that interaction left evidence of how you actually think. The corpus audit asks your AI tool to reflect on that record and identify patterns.

This is the piece that makes Leadership OS different from any assessment that came before it. Assessments ask you to describe yourself. The corpus audit asks the system that watched you work to describe what it saw.

What makes a strong corpus

  • Strategic or analytical work (not just "summarize this article")
  • Decision-making conversations
  • Communication drafts — emails, presentations, messages
  • Problems you worked through in real time
  • Recurring topics and themes over time
→
Minimum useful corpus: About 3 months of regular professional AI use. If your corpus is thin, complete the audit anyway and note the limitation. Return to it in 3–6 months when more history has accumulated — the profile will improve. Run the audit in the AI tool where most of your substantive professional conversations live.

The Corpus Audit Prompt

Copy the prompt below and paste it into your AI tool. Do not edit it. Run it in the same AI tool where most of your professional conversations live.

Corpus Audit Prompt — paste into your primary AI tool
I'd like you to reflect on our conversation history together. Do not summarize specific conversations. Instead, I want you to identify behavioral patterns — things you have observed repeatedly about how I think, work, and communicate. Please reflect on each of the following and share what you have noticed: 1. How do I tend to frame problems? What patterns do you see in how I approach complex or ambiguous situations? 2. What topics or domains come up most frequently in our conversations? Where does my thinking seem deepest and most developed? 3. How would you describe my communication style — the way I ask questions, express disagreement, give feedback on your outputs, and make requests of you? 4. What seems to energize me in our conversations? What kinds of work or problems do I gravitate toward? 5. What frustrates me? Where do you see friction, impatience, or repeated difficulty in how I engage? 6. What recurring questions or challenges keep coming up across our conversations? 7. What are my decision-making tendencies — how I evaluate tradeoffs, what I seem to prioritize, what I consistently avoid? 8. What do you notice about my relationship to structure, ambiguity, detail, and systems? Be specific and honest. I am not looking for flattery. I am looking for an accurate behavioral profile based on what you have actually observed across our conversations. If you are uncertain about something, say so rather than speculating.

Tool-specific instructions

ChatGPT
  • Paste in the account where most work conversations live
  • If you use Projects, paste inside the relevant project
  • ChatGPT's memory feature will draw on accumulated context
Claude
  • Paste in a conversation in Claude
  • If you use Projects, paste inside the project with the most relevant context
  • Claude will draw on project memory and shared files
Gemini
  • Paste into Gemini
  • Gemini Advanced draws on history associated with your Google account
  • Use the same Google account you use for work conversations

What to do with the output

01
Read the entire response
02
Note anything that surprises you — surprises are often the most useful findings
03
Note anything that seems inaccurate — you will correct it in Section 4
04
Copy the entire response into your notes. Label it: Corpus Audit Output — [Date]
05

Limitations to keep in mind

The corpus audit captures written professional communication. It may not represent how you communicate verbally or informally, how you behave under significant stress or conflict, or any part of your professional life handled without AI assistance. These are not failures of the methodology — they are honest limits of the evidence.

Section 03 · 35–40 minutes

Structured
Reflection.

Six prompts that surface what no assessment can reach — specific examples of how you actually operate under pressure, in decisions, and in relation to others.

Three ways to complete this section

Option A — AI Interview (recommended)
  • Paste the guided interview prompt into a new AI conversation
  • The AI asks one question at a time
  • Conversational reflection surfaces more than writing alone
  • Best for most people
Option B — Written
  • Answer each prompt in writing below or in a separate document
  • Best for precise, detail-oriented thinkers
  • Take your time — do not rush
Option C — Voice-to-Text
  • Record a voice memo answering each prompt
  • Use phone transcription or Otter.ai / Whisper
  • Paste transcript into notes or AI directly
  • Often produces richer, less edited responses

Prompt 1 — Peak Performance

Prompt 1 of 6
Describe a specific situation where you were operating at your best as a leader. What were you doing, who were you with, what were the conditions — and what made it different from ordinary work?
Purpose
Surfaces your Flow conditions — the specific combination of challenge, context, and collaboration that produces your best work. These conditions are often invisible until you examine them deliberately.
Good response
Specific. Names the situation, the people, the challenge level, and what made it feel different. Not "I love building things" — "During the Q4 system implementation, when the timeline compressed and stakeholders were pushing back, I found myself…" The conditions matter as much as the content.
Common mistakes
Describing a successful outcome instead of the conditions that produced it. Staying vague about what made it feel different. Choosing the most impressive story rather than the most honest one.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Prompt 2 — Decision Process

Prompt 2 of 6
Describe a decision you made in the past year that you are genuinely proud of. Not the outcome — the process. How did you get there?
Purpose
Reveals your decision architecture — how you gather information, stress-test assumptions, manage uncertainty, and commit. The process is where your actual tendencies live; the outcome is often noise.
Good response
Walks through the actual mechanics: what information you gathered, who you consulted, what alternatives you considered, what made you uncertain, and what made you commit. Should take 2–3 paragraphs.
Common mistakes
Describing the outcome rather than the process. Skipping the uncertainty ("I just knew what to do"). Choosing a decision that was actually easy.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Prompt 3 — A Decision You'd Make Differently

Prompt 3 of 6
Describe a decision or situation you would handle differently if you could do it again. What happened, what did you do, and what does that tell you about how you operate under pressure or uncertainty?
Purpose
This is where the most useful development material lives. The pattern across examples like this reveals the specific conditions under which your defaults work against you.
Good response
Names the situation specifically. Describes what you actually did. Then reflects on what it reveals about your tendencies — not "I should have been more patient" but "when the timeline compressed and I felt uncertain, I moved to premature closure rather than staying in the ambiguity."
Common mistakes
Attributing the difficulty entirely to external factors. Describing what you should have done without examining why you didn't. Choosing a situation where you were obviously wrong — makes the reflection feel performative.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Prompt 4 — Recurring Frustration

Prompt 4 of 6
What is a recurring frustration in how you work — something that comes up repeatedly with other people, with organizations, or with yourself? Be specific about what triggers it and how it tends to show up.
Purpose
Recurring frustrations are diagnostic. They reveal the gap between how you expect things to work and how they actually do — which tells you something important about your standards, defaults, and the environments where you are most and least effective.
Good response
Names the specific trigger, not just the general theme. "People who don't follow through" is a theme. "My peer who consistently agrees to deadlines in the meeting and then quietly lets them slip without flagging it early enough" is a trigger. The specificity reveals the mechanism.
Common mistakes
Staying so general that the frustration could apply to any leader. Avoiding the honest frustration and choosing something more acceptable. Describing frustrations without reflecting on your role in them.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Prompt 5 — Energy Sources

Prompt 5 of 6
When do you find the work genuinely energizing? Not "meaningful work" in the abstract — specific contexts, activities, types of people, kinds of problems. What is happening when you lose track of time?
Purpose
The input for your Flow profile — the specific conditions that produce your highest-quality engagement. The difference between "I love building things" and "I am most energized when I am three months into a new system build, the complexity has become clear, I have a team I trust, and stakeholders are engaged but not yet impatient" is the difference between a generic profile and a useful one.
Good response
Names the type of problem, the stage of the work, the team context, the stakes level. The more specific the better.
Common mistakes
Generic answers that could apply to any engaged leader. Confusing "work I am good at" with "work that genuinely energizes me" — these are sometimes different.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Prompt 6 — The Misread

Prompt 6 of 6
What do people consistently get wrong about you — as a leader, as a colleague, as a person? What does that misread tell you about the gap between how you operate and how you come across?
Purpose
The most revealing prompt in the set. The gap between intent and impact is where most leadership development work actually happens — and most leaders never examine it directly. People who can articulate this clearly have done real self-reflection.
Good response
Names a specific, recurring misread that creates friction or confusion. Then reflects on what produces it — the gap between how you experience yourself and how others receive you.
Common mistakes
Choosing a misread that reflects well on you ("people think I'm too demanding because I have high standards"). Avoiding the honest misread. Describing it without reflecting on what causes it.
Your response here — or respond in the AI interview below...

Section 04 · 15 minutes

Analysis
Engine.

This section combines all three evidence sources. One prompt teaches your AI the Leadership OS methodology. Then you provide your inputs and run the pattern analysis.

→
Start a fresh conversation. Open a new AI conversation for this section. Do not run it in the same conversation as the corpus audit or the guided interview. The fresh context ensures the AI is not confused about which inputs are framework instructions versus your evidence.

Step 4A — The Initialization Prompt

Copy the entire prompt below and paste it as your first message in a new AI conversation. Do not edit it. Wait for the AI to confirm it is ready before providing your evidence inputs.

Leadership OS Initialization Prompt — paste as first message in a new conversation
You are operating as a Leadership OS Analysis Engine for this session. Leadership OS is a multi-modal leadership reflection and development methodology. It combines three sources of evidence to produce a developmental profile: structured personality assessments, behavioral evidence from AI conversation history, and structured first-person reflection. WHAT LEADERSHIP OS IS: A structured process for making implicit leadership patterns explicit. A synthesis of multiple evidence sources into working documents. A developmental framework — not a measurement instrument. WHAT LEADERSHIP OS IS NOT: A psychometric assessment or validated instrument. A personality test or diagnostic tool. Executive coaching, therapy, or clinical assessment. A predictor of job performance or potential. YOUR ROLE: You are a synthesis engine, not a coach or evaluator. Your job is to identify patterns across evidence sources, express appropriate confidence in those patterns, and produce honest, specific working documents. THE THREE EVIDENCE MODALITIES: 1. ASSESSMENTS — Treat as useful hypotheses, not objective truth. Language: "The assessment suggests a tendency toward..." Never: "You are..." or "This proves..." 2. BEHAVIORAL EVIDENCE — Treat as observed patterns, not trait indicators. Language: "A pattern appears across your work of..." Never: "Your behavior proves that you..." 3. STRUCTURED REFLECTION — Treat as meaning-making and self-awareness signal. Language: "In your reflection, you describe..." Never: "Your reflection reveals the truth about..." CONFIDENCE LEVELS — Label every pattern with one of these four levels: HIGH CONFIDENCE: Three sources converge on the same pattern. Language: "The evidence consistently shows..." MODERATE CONFIDENCE: Two sources converge. Language: "The evidence suggests..." EMERGING HYPOTHESIS: One source points toward a pattern. Language: "Worth exploring whether..." SPECULATIVE: Theoretical inference only — no direct evidence. Language: "A question this raises is..." Never state a conclusion without its confidence level. Never blur these categories. Never use certainty language for anything below high confidence. CONFLICT RULE: When evidence sources contradict each other, flag the contradiction explicitly. Do not force coherence. Name the tension as a question worth exploring. Tension between sources is often the most informative finding. GOVERNANCE: Never claim validity, reliability, or normative comparison for any finding. Never make predictive or causal claims. Never use diagnostic or clinical language. At least one limitation must be acknowledged in every synthesis document. When you are ready, confirm that you understand the Leadership OS framework and tell me you are ready to receive the evidence inputs. I will provide them one at a time.

Section 04b · 10 minutes

Construct
Analysis.

The intermediate step between evidence and outputs. Before generating documents, the AI evaluates each Leadership OS construct across all three evidence sources and builds a working model of how you operate.

What construct analysis is

A construct is a specific dimension of leadership behavior — like Decision Style or Self-Awareness — that Leadership OS is designed to surface. Construct Analysis is the process of evaluating the evidence you have provided against each of these dimensions and generating hypotheses about how you operate within each one.

This is not producing personality scores. It is not assigning you to a type. The AI is weighing evidence across three modalities — assessment, behavior, and reflection — identifying where they converge and where they conflict, and expressing appropriate confidence in each finding. The result is a working model of your leadership patterns, not a definitive description of who you are.

The nine Leadership OS constructs

Reflection Orientation · Systems Orientation · Learning Orientation · Decision Style · Communication Patterns · Leadership Identity · Adaptability · Self-Awareness · Development Readiness

For each one, the AI will identify: what the evidence shows, where sources agree or conflict, and how confident it is in the finding.

→
Stay in the same conversation as Section 4. The AI already has your evidence inputs and the initialization context. Send the construct analysis prompt as the next message in that same conversation.

The Construct Analysis Prompt

Send this as your next message after the pattern analysis in Section 4. The AI will evaluate all nine constructs across your three evidence sources.

Construct Analysis Prompt — send after the pattern analysis
Now please conduct a Leadership OS Construct Analysis. Using all three evidence sources — assessment results, corpus audit output, and reflection responses — evaluate each of the nine Leadership OS constructs. For each construct, provide: CONSTRUCT NAME Definition (one sentence — what this construct measures) Evidence from assessments: what the assessment data suggests Evidence from behavioral corpus: what the observed patterns show Evidence from reflection: what the leader's own account reveals Convergence or conflict: do the sources agree? If not, what is the tension? Confidence level: HIGH / MODERATE / EMERGING / INSUFFICIENT Developmental implication: what this means for how this leader operates THE NINE CONSTRUCTS TO EVALUATE: 1. REFLECTION ORIENTATION How deliberately does this leader examine their own experience and draw developmental insight from it? 2. SYSTEMS ORIENTATION How naturally does this leader frame problems in terms of structures, interdependencies, and root causes? 3. LEARNING ORIENTATION How readily does this leader seek new information, tolerate uncertainty, and treat experience as developmental material? 4. DECISION STYLE What is this leader's characteristic approach to decisions under uncertainty and competing priorities? 5. COMMUNICATION PATTERNS What are this leader's characteristic ways of structuring, delivering, and receiving communication? 6. LEADERSHIP IDENTITY How does this leader understand their role and what kind of leader they are becoming? 7. ADAPTABILITY How readily does this leader adjust approach, strategy, or behavior in response to changing conditions? 8. SELF-AWARENESS How accurately does this leader's model of themselves match what the evidence shows about how they actually operate? 9. DEVELOPMENT READINESS What is this leader's current capacity and motivation for deliberate development work? CONFIDENCE LEVELS: HIGH CONFIDENCE — three sources converge on the same pattern MODERATE CONFIDENCE — two sources converge EMERGING HYPOTHESIS — one source points toward a pattern INSUFFICIENT EVIDENCE — not enough evidence to form a useful hypothesis After evaluating all nine constructs, provide a brief LEADERSHIP PROFILE SUMMARY: A 200–300 word synthesis of the most important patterns across all constructs. This should read as a working model of how this leader operates — honest, specific, and grounded in the evidence. Note the two or three constructs where evidence is strongest. Note any constructs where the evidence was insufficient or contradictory. Frame everything as hypotheses, not facts. This profile is a starting point, not a finished description.

The Leadership Profile artifact

The construct analysis output is your Leadership Profile — the intermediate layer between your evidence inputs and your output documents. It is not a document you share externally. It is the working model your AI will use to generate the four output documents in Section 5.

The Leadership Profile is private by design. It contains the full evidence reasoning — including tension points, low-confidence hypotheses, and developmental implications — that you may not want to share in full. The output documents (User Manual, Working-With-Me Guide) are the edited, shareable versions derived from this profile.

Document 1 — Leadership User Manual

Leadership User Manual Prompt
Based on the evidence we have analyzed — the assessment results, the corpus audit, the reflection responses, and the pattern analysis — please write my Leadership User Manual. Format: 3 to 4 pages. First person ("I"). Structured with clear section headers. Required sections: HOW I THINK My cognitive patterns, how I frame problems, how I approach decisions. What my thinking style looks like from the inside and the outside. HOW I ENGAGE How I operate with people — in collaboration, in conflict, under pressure. How I give feedback, receive feedback, and lead. WHAT PRODUCES MY BEST WORK My flow conditions. The specific combination of challenge, context, and relationship that brings out my most effective work. WHERE I CREATE FRICTION The specific conditions under which my natural tendencies work against me. Be honest. This section is often the most useful. MY GOVERNING QUESTIONS The 2 to 5 questions that appear to drive my decisions. Extracted from the pattern analysis, not invented. HOW TO WORK WITH ME What I need from collaborators. How to bring me a problem. How to challenge me effectively. What to watch for when I am struggling. Write this as a document I could share with a new manager, a new team member, or a trusted colleague. Not a psychological report — a practical guide to how I operate. After generating the document, flag any section where the evidence was thin or where you are drawing on emerging hypotheses rather than high-confidence patterns.

Document 2 — Working-With-Me Guide

Working-With-Me Guide Prompt
Now please write my Working-With-Me Guide. Format: One page. Five short sections. TO BRING ME A PROBLEM What you should bring, what you don't need to bring, and how to frame it so I can engage effectively. TO CHALLENGE ME EFFECTIVELY How I receive pushback. What works. What doesn't. How to disagree with me productively. WHAT I NEED FROM YOU The 2 to 3 things that make the biggest difference in how well I can work with someone. WHAT YOU CAN EXPECT FROM ME How I show up. What I will deliver. What I hold myself to. WHEN I AM STRUGGLING — WHAT TO WATCH FOR The signals that something is wrong that might not be obvious. Be specific — behavioral specifics are more useful than general signals. Tone: Concrete and direct. Readable in under 3 minutes. No abstractions. Every sentence should be specific to me — if it could apply to any leader, rewrite it.

Document 3 — AI Calibration Document

AI Calibration Document Prompt
Now please write my AI Calibration Document. This is the document I will paste at the start of any new AI conversation to help the AI understand how I work and produce better outputs faster. Format: One page. Dense but scannable. Required sections: MY COMMUNICATION STYLE AND VOICE How I write. What I find useful versus generic. What makes a response feel like it understands me versus feels like it was written for anyone. MY OPERATING PRINCIPLES The 3 to 5 non-negotiables in how I work. What I will not compromise on. WHAT GOOD OUTPUT LOOKS LIKE TO ME Specific. What distinguishes an output I will use from one I will rewrite. Format, tone, structure, level of detail. MY GOVERNING QUESTION The single question I run most decisions through. This should inform how the AI frames tradeoffs in its outputs. Document 4 — Development Roadmap (private)

Development Roadmap Prompt — private document, not for sharing
Finally, please write my Development Roadmap. This is a private document — not for sharing. Be honest. This document serves only me. Format: 2 to 3 pages. What should evolve vs. remain stable

Evolves
  • Development Roadmap — every 90 days
  • Monitoring questions — as old ones become automatic
  • Behavioral examples — add new ones while fresh
  • AI Calibration Document — as you evolve
Stable
  • Core trait profile — changes slowly
  • Flow conditions — usually consistent across years
  • Governing principles — the non-negotiables
  • Previous versions — keep them, the evolution matters
Retire
  • Development edges you have genuinely resolved
  • Behavioral examples that no longer feel representative
  • Monitoring questions you no longer need
  • Document sections experience has proven consistently inaccurate
Section 07 · Optional

Research
Participation.

An optional invitation — not a requirement, not a condition of access. Completely your choice.

Leadership OS is an early-stage methodology. Understanding whether it produces useful insights across different leaders, roles, and contexts requires more than one person's experience. If you are willing, you can optionally contribute anonymized data from your session to a research dataset.

What would be collected (if you choose to participate):

Your role level, industry, function, and approximate years leading people · Organization size · Which evidence sources you completed · A summary of your construct profile — for example, "high confidence Systems Orientation, moderate confidence Adaptability" — not your actual responses or documents · Which reflection mode you used

What would never be collected:

Your name, organization, or any identifying information · Your reflection responses or interview transcript · Your corpus audit output · Your assessment results · Your output documents · Any content that could identify you or your organization

Why it matters

The most interesting question Leadership OS is positioned to investigate is whether the gap between self-reported assessment scores and AI-observed behavioral patterns is meaningful and measurable across a large population. That question cannot be answered from one person's experience. It requires data from many.

How to participate

If you would like to contribute your anonymized construct profile, send an email to mikejcilla@gmail.com with the subject line Leadership OS Research Participation and include the items listed under "What would be collected" above.

You can withdraw your participation at any time by emailing the same address.
